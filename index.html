G'day!

I am an Associate Professor in Project Management at the School of Built Environment, University of Technology Sydney, Australia. My name is pronounced "yong-jyen kuh".

A position

I study why technically competent and economically justified projects still face resistance, delay, and loss of trust, and what this reveals about how projects are governed.

For me, project delivery is not only technical execution. It is a social and institutional process. Contracts, procurement rules, performance measures, and engagement practices shape behaviour. They also shape legitimacy, credibility, and public value over time.

My research agenda

My research frames projects as temporary governance arrangements rather than neutral delivery vehicles. This means I focus on design choices made early, because they often lock in incentives, accountability, and stakeholder relationships that later become hard to change.

I work across Australian and Chinese contexts. I use this comparative lens to make governance assumptions visible, and to test whether common project management ideas travel well across institutions.

What I am known for

A large part of my work centres on public private partnerships and long-term delivery models. I examine risk allocation and contracting behaviour, including how performance-based settings influence decision-making, adaptability, and long-term outcomes.

I also focus on legitimacy and social outcomes in infrastructure delivery. I treat social licence and social sustainability as project conditions that can be built, damaged, and rebuilt, rather than a one-off communication task.

More recently, I have extended this thinking to digitalisation and AI in project settings. I am interested in project-level readiness, governance guardrails, and what changes when decisions are supported by data-driven tools.

Credibility, without the noise

I publish in leading journals in project management and the built environment. I have authored over 100 journal papers, with more than 7,900 citations and an h-index of 36. I have been recognised among the Stanford-listed Top 2% Scientist.
